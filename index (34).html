How I Work

I align teams around a consistent approach to revenue systems. This becomes the operating system for how product, engineering, and analytics manage revenue performance. This approach consistently identifies and prioritizes the highest-impact revenue recovery opportunities within weeks.

01

Map the revenue flow

From authorization to collection to retention. Understand the full lifecycle before touching any metric.

02

Identify where revenue is lost

Declines, retries, identity state, and lifecycle gaps. Most of the loss is concentrated in a small number of failure modes.

Classify failures

What is recoverable versus terminal. You cannot optimize what you have not classified.

04

Volume tells you what is common. Recoverable revenue tells you what matters. These are rarely the same list.

Case Studies

Payments Revenue

Global Billthrough & Net Revenue Program

+

I owned a cross-functional program across analytics, engineering, and operations to redesign how revenue performance was measured and improved. I led the design and prioritization of the measurement framework — the decline taxonomy, the recoverable revenue model, and the market-level performance standards. I partnered with Product and Engineering to execute the intervention roadmap, influencing prioritization decisions across teams who did not report to me.

Authorization rates varied dramatically by market, carrier, and user segment, and most revenue loss was uncategorized. At scale, a 1pp change in billthrough is not a metric improvement. It is a material change in revenue.

The primary constraint was decline classification, which determined speed of diagnosis and revenue recovery. Without classification, prioritization is guesswork.

System foundation: Unified decline taxonomy across markets and carriers. Most revenue loss was uncategorized. The gap between what authorizes and what collects is where material revenue is lost, and it is invisible until you classify it. Once every failure was classified as retryable, actionable, or terminal, the opportunity became quantifiable.

Decision framework: Prioritized by recoverable revenue, not volume. The constraint was limited engineering capacity. We chose to optimize for decline classification accuracy first, accepting that retry timing improvements would come second. That decision alone changed where the business invested. Billthrough improvement ranged from +2pp to +10pp depending on market and decline reason. A single global average would have obscured the variance that drove the prioritization decisions.

Expansion: Linked payment performance to identity state.

Authentication, account condition, and lifecycle behavior determine recovery and retention.

This shifted the problem from payments optimization to system-level revenue recovery. The program recovered over $100M in revenue and recovered 50,000+ subscribers from passive churn across major merchants including Spotify, Amazon, Sony, Microsoft, and Netflix. This required close partnership with Engineering, Product, Operations, merchants, and issuers at every stage.

Revenue Intelligence Infrastructure

Building a Self-Serve Revenue Intelligence Foundation

+

When I took on responsibility for business analytics and revenue optimization, the function was primarily reactive. It produced reports on request rather than enabling the business to answer its own questions. I led a multi-year effort to transform it into a self-serve analytics platform that shifted ownership of standard reporting to the business and freed the team to focus on higher-leverage decisions.

This involved redesigning the data model for payments and subscription data, rebuilding core dashboards for performance and cost, establishing data quality checks and observability for key billing flows, and developing standards for how the team reviews and ships analytical work. The constraint was stakeholder trust. Teams were running their own numbers and arriving at different answers. We chose to fix definition alignment before building anything new. Without that foundation, every dashboard becomes a negotiation. A critical part of the work was establishing canonical KPI definitions for revenue, transactions, billthrough, auth rate, capture rate, and declines. The result was a function that scaled without proportional headcount growth and shifted the team's time from reporting to decision-making.

Issuer Analytics Strategy

Issuer Performance Analytics Program

+

Issuer performance is one of the most underinstrumented dimensions in subscription payments analytics. Authorization rates, decline patterns, and recovery rates vary significantly by issuer, and without visibility into that layer, optimization efforts target the wrong variables. I built the issuer performance analytics program at Boku, giving the team and merchant partners visibility into performance by issuer, market, and decline reason.

Alongside that I built a daily C-suite facing KPI dashboard that showed performance versus the prior month on a running total basis and surfaced which merchant, market, and issuer combination was driving any change and why. Executives could see the number move and immediately understand the root cause without additional analysis. That closed the loop between data and decision at the speed the business needed.

How I Use AI

AI has not changed what I think analysts are for. It has changed what analysts spend their time on. The shift is from execution to judgment — from producing outputs to evaluating them, from running analysis to defining what is worth analyzing.

In practice I use AI in three ways. For exploratory analysis, it compresses the time between a question and a hypothesis. I can surface patterns in a dataset, stress-test a framework, or pressure-test an assumption in minutes rather than hours. The judgment about what the pattern means and whether it matters is still mine. AI makes the exploration faster, not the interpretation better.

For building tools and prototypes, AI functions as a coding collaborator. This entire portfolio — the site, the seven interactive tools, the payments stack audit with its sixty pre-written diagnostic narratives — was built in Claude without a traditional engineering workflow. That is not a demonstration of what AI can do. It is a demonstration of what an analytically rigorous operator can build when execution is no longer the constraint.

For thinking through analytical frameworks, I use AI as a thinking partner rather than a source of answers. It is useful for stress-testing a proposed metric definition, identifying what a framework is missing, or articulating a position more precisely. The framework design is always mine. The AI accelerates the refinement.

Building an AI-first analytics function requires more infrastructure investment than a traditional one, not less. When every analyst on the team can generate outputs in minutes, the leverage of the person who defines and enforces the measurement infrastructure increases proportionally. Canonical definitions, instrumentation standards, and metric governance become the critical investment because they are the shared foundation that makes AI-generated analysis consistent and trustworthy across the team. Without that foundation, AI produces faster disagreements. The analyst who owns the trust layer becomes the most leveraged person in the function.

The risk in analytics is not that AI replaces analysts. It is that teams treat AI-generated outputs as analysis and stop asking whether the question was right in the first place. That is where trust infrastructure matters most: canonical definitions, instrumentation standards, and metric governance that ensure everyone is asking the same question before AI helps answer it faster.

The most forward-looking version of this work is the analytics team building and maintaining the AI instruction layer for the entire organization. In practice this means owning the Skills.md files — prompt artifacts that encode canonical KPI definitions, metric logic, and measurement standards into the AI tools every team uses. When an engineer asks Claude Code to write a revenue query, when a PM asks an AI assistant to pull conversion data, when finance asks for a churn report — the Skills.md is what ensures every output uses the same definition of billthrough, the same cohort logic, the same churn calculation. Canonical definitions stop being documentation that people may or may not read. They become machine-readable and enforceable at the point of generation. The analytics team that builds and maintains that layer owns something that scales across every AI-assisted workflow in the company — not just the ones their team runs directly.

Leadership

An analytics team earns its seat at the table by being the most trustworthy voice in the room, not the fastest one. When everyone can generate a number in minutes, the job is not analysis. It is ensuring the organization asks the right question and gets the same answer every time.

I lead from the data. I write SQL, review schemas, and debug dashboards alongside my team. That is what earns the credibility to push back on flawed assumptions and develop analysts who trust your judgment.

At Boku I built a revenue analytics function that went from reactive report production to a self-serve platform trusted by product, finance, and operations across 60+ markets. The most important work was not the dashboards. It was the instrumentation, the metric governance, and the decline taxonomy. That is trust infrastructure. That is what I build.

My focus is shortening the cycle from question to decision. I define clear ownership between product, engineering, and analytics so teams can move quickly without ambiguity. The goal is not just better analysis, but an organization that consistently makes better decisions.
