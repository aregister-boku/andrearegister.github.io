Payments Strategy · Analysis

The Authorization Rate
Battle

How the most overlooked metric in payments became the competitive fault line between issuers, acquirers, networks, and the processors who decided to do something about it.

Andrea Register
Director, Business Analytics
Payments & Identity · Analytics
March 2026

A customer reaches checkout. Card declined. They try again. Declined again. They close the tab. No fraud occurred. A real customer, a valid card, available funds. Gone. The merchant absorbed the loss. The issuer gave up the interchange. Every party in the chain paid a price — and not one of them is formally accountable for it.

I have watched this happen, at scale, across multiple markets, for more than a decade. The part that never stops being remarkable is this: the industry built an entire discipline around the fraud it loses to. It built almost nothing around the revenue it throws away itself.

That is finally changing. This piece examines how, why it took so long, and what it means analytically for anyone working in subscription payments.

01 —

A Decision Made in the Dark

Every card payment triggers a relay race across five parties in under two seconds. The customer initiates. The gateway validates. The acquirer formats and forwards. The card network applies its intelligence layer. The issuer makes the final call. Then the whole thing runs back in reverse, ending in an approval or a two-digit code that means no.

That relay sounds clean. It is not.

The merchant at the start of the chain holds the richest signal imaginable: device fingerprint, session behaviour, IP address, browsing history, account age, purchase context. By the time that signal reaches the issuer, much of it has been stripped, garbled, or compressed beyond recognition. Free-form merchant descriptions get truncated. MCC codes collapse entire business categories into four-digit shorthand.

"Yum Brands" tells you nothing about whether this is a $6 KFC order or a $400 catering invoice.

Here is what that signal loss looks like in practice. An issuer's model sees a transaction from an unfamiliar merchant descriptor, a card used in a different geography than usual, at an amount slightly above average. Three soft risk signals, none individually decisive. But together, with no behavioural context to offset them, the model declines. The customer is on a business trip. The merchant is a hotel. The card is perfectly valid. Nobody ever finds out what happened.

The Opacity Problem

Research by Checkout.com and Oxford Economics found that 50% of merchants don't receive raw response codes when a payment fails. 67% don't receive fraud or chargeback analysis from their PSPs at all. The issuer, making the most consequential decision in the transaction, is working with the least context about what's actually happening. And when the answer is no, the message sent back is usually "Do Not Honor" — three words, no explanation, no feedback loop, no accountability.

02 —

How Incentives Built the Problem

This is not a story about incompetence. The over-decline problem is the entirely predictable output of a deeply irrational incentive structure. Most analysis of this topic stops at the surface. The underlying arithmetic is worth working through precisely.

When issuer risk teams are walked through this arithmetic, the response is usually a version of the same thing: "We know." They know. The incentive structure is not invisible to the people inside it. The institutional gravity of accountability for fraud is so much stronger than the diffuse, unmeasured cost of a declined legitimate transaction that the rational individual response — inside that institution — is to optimise for what they are measured on.

27% of all declines originate from issuers. The most common decline code across the industry is "Do Not Honor" — a catch-all that tells nobody anything, and exists precisely because specificity creates accountability.

The system did not break. It worked exactly as designed. That is the problem.

03 —

The Spiral Nobody Measured

The problem does not stay static. It compounds — and it does so in a way that is genuinely difficult to detect unless you are looking at the right data. The mechanism documented by Ethoca, a Mastercard subsidiary, works as follows:

1
Chargebacks coded as fraud in training data
Friendly fraud and legitimate disputes get labelled as fraud in the issuer's ML training pipeline. The model cannot distinguish between "I didn't authorise this" and "I forgot I subscribed."
↓
2
Models become more conservative
The model learns that certain merchant categories, geographies, or transaction patterns are riskier than they actually are. The blunt response — tighten the rules — is fast and defensible. The surgical response requires data infrastructure many institutions don't have.
↓
3
More legitimate transactions declined
Merchants in the affected categories see billthrough rates fall. Frustrated subscribers churn. The merchant loses revenue. The issuer loses interchange. The network loses volume. No one reports it.
↓
4
Training data shifts further toward fraud signal
Legitimate transactions disappear from the data. The ratio of fraudulent to legitimate transactions in the training corpus worsens. The model tightens further. The spiral continues.

The Insidious Part

The false positive spiral feels like the system is working. Fraud losses down. Chargeback ratios clean. Regulatory posture solid. The merchant churning quietly in the background is someone else's problem. Alloy's 2025 State of Fraud report found 99% of financial institutions now use ML as part of fraud prevention. That headline sounds like progress. "Using ML" describes a spectrum running from full neural network-driven authorisation to a static rule set with an ML layer that the rules can override whenever a risk manager feels uncomfortable. The sophistication gap between institutions is enormous. It just does not show up in the metric that gets reported.

04 —

What This Actually Costs

Numbers in this space need careful handling. The most cited figure is $443 billion — the gross value of legitimate card-not-present transactions falsely declined globally, from an Aite Group report commissioned by ClearSale in 2020. That figure represents gross transaction value, not merchant losses, and has not been updated since 2021 despite global e-commerce growing roughly 70% in the intervening period.

A more methodologically grounded figure comes from Checkout.com and Oxford Economics. Their September 2023 research measured $50.7 billion in direct merchant false decline losses across the US, UK, France, and Germany — four markets only — representing a 140% increase from their 2020 estimate of $20.3 billion for the same four markets.

05 —

When the Networks Started Paying Attention

The networks saw the problem first, because they are the only parties in the chain with enough volume to see the patterns that individual institutions cannot. Visa now processes 257.5 billion transactions annually. Mastercard processes 159.4 billion. At that scale, the signal that is invisible to any individual issuer becomes statistically obvious.

The result was a shift in what network intelligence is actually for. Visa's Advanced Authorization evaluates 500+ risk attributes per transaction in under a millisecond. Mastercard's Decision Intelligence generates predictive risk scores using behavioural signals, geo-data, spending patterns, and device information. These products are positioned as fraud tools. Their primary practical function is authorisation intelligence. The distinction matters because it changes how institutions should be thinking about them strategically.

The Tokenisation Signal

Network tokenisation proved the broader point in hard data. A network token signals to the issuer that the credential is fresh, the merchant relationship is legitimate, and the card has not been compromised. That signal changes the issuer's risk calculation before any fraud model runs. The credential infrastructure shift was not just a security upgrade. It was a signal quality upgrade. Better credentials produce better decisions. This pattern runs through the entire authorisation optimisation story.

What Stripe and Adyen demonstrate is not that you need their specific scale to solve this problem. What they demonstrate is that authorisation optimisation, built deliberately, using the data you have, on top of the network signals available to you, produces measurable commercial outcomes. They treated authorisation rate as a product problem and invested accordingly.

The Architectural Insight

At most acquirers, retry logic, routing decisions, and fraud scoring operate independently. Each feature does its job. None of them informs the others. At the best acquirers, everything shares context. The Stripe/Twilio A/B test across multiple major PSPs is the most methodologically transparent example: +10% total auth rate uplift broken down as +5.5% from infrastructure, +1% from Adaptive Acceptance, +2% from Card Account Updater, +1.5% from consulting. That is not one big feature. It is five levers compounding. Individual features add. Orchestrated features compound.

07 —

A Widening Gap and What It Means

The industry is splitting into two groups. Institutions that have treated authorisation intelligence as a strategic capability — and institutions that are still treating it as an operational footnote. The distance between them, measured in basis points, is now large enough to show up in revenue and in competitive position.

The Gap — Authorisation Rate by Institution Type (Comparable Profiles) Industry analysis · PSP and acquirer research
Institutions Ahead
Authorization as a product discipline
Dedicated auth optimization teams. ML models across the full payment funnel. Features that share context — retry logic informed by fraud scores, routing informed by real-time PSP performance. Continuous experimentation. Measurable commercial accountability for basis point movements.
Institutions Behind
Authorization as operational plumbing
Static rule sets with an ML layer that rules can override. Retry logic that resubmits the same data to the same endpoint. No decline taxonomy. No retry cost awareness. Features that exist but cannot talk to each other. No commercial accountability for false decline rates.

European banks got a preview of what this pressure looks like when it arrives all at once. CMSPI's analysis of the PSD2 SCA enforcement period found a weighted average 30% failure rate on 3DS2-challenged transactions, with potential annual merchant losses of €86 billion across Europe. The adaptation was bifurcated. Tier-1 merchants with sophisticated PSP partners achieved 98% exemption request honour rates. Smaller merchants and issuers in Southern and Eastern Europe were still recovering years later.

SCA was a compliance event that became an authorisation stress test. The institutions that understood it as an optimisation problem adapted. Those that treated it as a compliance checkbox are still paying for that choice.

08 —

What Comes Next: The Agentic Problem

The next frontier is not an incremental improvement on existing optimisation. It is a structural disruption to the signal infrastructure the entire system depends on.

AI agents transacting autonomously produce no mouse movements, no session histories, no device fingerprints in any conventional sense. The entire behavioural signal infrastructure that current authorisation systems depend on does not exist for machine-initiated transactions. Visa launched its Trusted Agent Protocol in October 2025. Mastercard unveiled Agent Pay in April 2025. The industry is already trying to solve a problem it has not yet fully priced into its current infrastructure decisions.

The Agentic Challenge for Subscription Analytics

Subscription payments are already the highest-trust, lowest-friction transaction type in card payments — recurring, merchant-initiated, credentialed. Agentic transactions will look similar from an issuer's perspective: no human session, no device behaviour, no browsing context. The analytics and optimisation frameworks being built today for subscription billing will become the foundation for modelling agentic payment legitimacy. The institutions investing in authorisation intelligence now are building the data foundation they will need for the next problem.

The Analytical Imperative

The authorisation rate has always been the most important number in payments that nobody was treating as important. That is finally changing — pushed by network penalties from above, merchant defection from below, and a generation of processors who proved the commercial case by building it.

The tools now exist at every level of the stack. Network scoring signals, tokenisation infrastructure, 3DS 2.x data richness, and ML-informed retry logic are all accessible to institutions willing to integrate them properly. The gap for most players is not access to the tools. It is the absence of a strategy to use them. And that is a choice, not a constraint.

For subscription merchants specifically, the compounding economics make this the highest-leverage investment available. A single percentage point of authorisation rate improvement at €1 billion volume recovers €10 million in revenue — at zero incremental customer acquisition cost. The customer already chose to pay. The question is whether the infrastructure lets them.
