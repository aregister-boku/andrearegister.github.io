The Compounding Principle

The Stripe/Twilio case study showed a 10% total uplift from five levers: +5.5% infrastructure, +1% Adaptive Acceptance, +2% Card Account Updater, +1.5% consulting. Individual features add linearly. Orchestrated features compound — each lever improves the signal quality available to the others.

The Network Data Moat & AI Cost Model

Two underweighted factors in the PSP vs. direct decision — what you lose by leaving the PSP network, and whether AI-assisted development actually changes the build economics.

What You Give Up Leaving the PSP Network

The Moat You Can't Train Around

Stripe's fraud model is trained on $1.9T of annual transaction volume across millions of merchants globally. When a fraudster tests a stolen card on a small e-commerce site, that signal propagates across the entire network within hours. A sole trader on Stripe gets the same fraud intelligence as the Fortune 100. Going direct means your fraud model is trained on your data only — and your data is a rounding error relative to the network. AI can help you build the model. It cannot give you the training data.

Does AI Change the Build vs. Buy Math?

The Honest Answer

AI-assisted development genuinely compresses integration time. What took 12–18 months of payments engineering 5 years ago can now be scaffolded in 6–8 weeks with Claude Code or Cursor. That changes the economics of the code. It does not change the economics of compliance, scheme membership, chargeback liability, issuer relationship management, or the operational burden of being a direct participant. The code was never the expensive part. The obligations were.
