Case Studies

Payments Revenue

Global Billthrough & Net Revenue Program

+

Subscription merchants on carrier billing networks face a unique challenge: authorization rates vary dramatically by market, carrier, and user segment. Small changes in billthrough rate compound across millions of transactions into significant revenue impact. I led an end-to-end analytics program to diagnose, prioritize, and measure improvements to billthrough and net revenue across Boku's global merchant base.

The program involved building a unified metric framework across markets and carriers, identifying the primary drivers of authorization failure by decline reason, designing and measuring retry and dunning experiments, and partnering with product and engineering to instrument key billing events. The analytical foundation enabled both reactive diagnosis and proactive optimization at scale.

Building a Self-Serve Analytics Foundation

+

When I took on the Former Director of Business Analytics role, the BI function was primarily reactive — producing reports on request rather than enabling the business to answer its own questions. I led a multi-year effort to transform it into a self-serve platform that product, finance, and operations teams could use independently.

This involved redesigning the data model for payments and subscription data, rebuilding core dashboards for performance and cost, establishing data quality checks and observability for key billing flows, and developing best practices for how the team reviews and ships analytical work. The result was a function that scaled without proportional headcount growth.

PSP Analytics Strategy

Payment Service Provider Performance Framework

+

Evaluating payment service providers requires balancing authorization rate performance, cost per transaction, fraud and chargeback exposure, and market coverage — often with inconsistent or incomplete data across providers. I built the analytics framework Boku used to evaluate, compare, and make routing and vendor decisions across its PSP relationships.

The framework standardized metrics across PSPs, built automated reporting on volume share and performance trends, and produced clear executive narratives for commercial and product decisions. It became the analytical backbone of Boku's PSP strategy conversations.

Leadership

My leadership style is built on a simple conviction: an analytics team earns its seat at the table by being the most trustworthy voice in the room, not the fastest one. In a world where everyone can generate a number in minutes, the analyst's job has shifted from producing analysis to ensuring that when the organization asks a question, everyone gets the same answer — and that answer is right.

I lead from the data. I write SQL alongside my team, review schemas, and debug broken dashboards. That technical depth is not about being hands-on for its own sake — it is what makes it possible to push back on flawed assumptions, develop analysts who trust your feedback, and earn credibility with engineering and finance when the analysis is contested.

At Boku I built a BI function that went from reactive report production to a self-serve platform trusted by product, finance, and operations across 60+ markets. The most important work was not the dashboards. It was the instrumentation, the metric governance, and the decline taxonomy that made everyone's subsequent analysis consistent and comparable. That is trust infrastructure. That is what I build.

Leadership Philosophy

"The best analytics functions don't just answer questions — they change what questions get asked. I build teams that make the business more curious."

01 —

Data quality is a product, not a task

Dashboards are only as valuable as the data behind them. I treat observability, instrumentation, and data quality as first-class deliverables, not afterthoughts. A team that owns its data quality earns the trust of the business.

02 —

Analysts should change decisions, not decorate them

The goal of analytics work is not a report. It is a better outcome. I hold my teams to a standard where every piece of work traces back to a decision that was changed or a question that was answered differently because of it.

03 —

Senior leaders earn credibility by going deep

I stay close to the data. I can write SQL, debug a broken dashboard, and review a schema alongside my team. That technical credibility is what makes it possible to push back on stakeholders, spot flawed assumptions, and develop analysts who trust your feedback.

04 —

Build for the team that comes after you

Good analytics infrastructure, clear documentation, and strong analyst development compound over time. I build functions designed to outlast my direct involvement, where the systems and people are strong enough to run without me.
