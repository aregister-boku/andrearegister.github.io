01 —

Problem Framing

Billthrough rate — the percentage of subscription billing attempts that successfully authorize and complete — is the single most commercially important metric in carrier billing. A billing attempt that fails doesn't just represent lost revenue in the moment; in subscription models, it triggers churn that compounds over the lifetime of the customer.

The challenge isn't that billthrough failure is invisible. It's that failure is distributed across hundreds of combinations of market, carrier, merchant, billing cycle, and decline reason — making it easy to see the aggregate problem and very hard to know where to act first.

The initial diagnostic question was simple: is low billthrough a data problem, a product problem, or a market problem? The answer turned out to be all three in different proportions depending on where you looked.

Framing Principle

Before prioritizing any intervention, we needed a metric framework that was consistent across markets and carriers — one where a "76% billthrough rate" in India meant the same thing as "76%" in Germany. Inconsistent event tracking and carrier reporting made this harder than it sounds.

02 —

Identifying the Key Drivers

The diagnostic phase started with decline reason analysis — decomposing every failed billing attempt into its root cause. This sounds straightforward; in practice it required significant data work, because carrier decline codes are inconsistent across networks, often mapped to generic buckets, and sometimes missing entirely.

The first analytical investment was building a unified decline taxonomy that translated carrier-specific codes into standardized categories we could analyze consistently. This alone took several weeks of work with the data engineering team, but it was what made everything else possible.

Methodology — Decline Classification

Each decline event was classified into one of four primary buckets: Insufficient Funds (user genuinely cannot pay at that moment), Technical / Carrier (network or routing failure, not user-driven), Fraud / Risk Block (carrier or merchant risk rule triggered), and Configuration Error (incorrect billing parameters, timing issues, or merchant setup problems).

The critical analytical distinction was between retryable declines — where a subsequent attempt at the right time has a materially higher success probability — and terminal declines, where retrying wastes cost and risks carrier penalties. Misclassifying these has direct revenue and cost implications.

Key Finding

62% of declines were retryable — but only 31% were actually being retried, and retry timing was not optimized for success probability. The largest single opportunity was not acquiring new volume; it was recovering existing volume that was already being lost.

SQL — Decline Driver Analysis by Market & Carrier

-- Identify retryable decline rate and current retry coverage by market
WITH decline_base AS (
  SELECT
    market,
    carrier_id,
    decline_category,
    is_retryable,
    COUNT(*) AS total_declines,
    SUM(CASE WHEN retry_attempted = 1 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS retries_attempted,
    SUM(CASE WHEN retry_succeeded = 1 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS retries_succeeded
  FROM billing_events
  WHERE event_type = 'decline'
    AND billing_date BETWEEN '2022-01-01' AND '2022-12-31'
  GROUP BY 1,2,3,4
),
opportunity AS (
  SELECT
    market,
    carrier_id,
    SUM(total_declines) AS total_declines,
    SUM(CASE WHEN is_retryable = 1 THEN total_declines ELSE 0 END) AS retryable_declines,
    SUM(retries_attempted) AS retries_attempted,
    SAFE_DIVIDE(
      SUM(retries_succeeded),
      NULLIF(SUM(retries_attempted), 0)
    ) AS retry_success_rate,
    -- Revenue at stake: retryable but not retried × avg txn value
    (SUM(CASE WHEN is_retryable = 1 THEN total_declines ELSE 0 END)
      - SUM(retries_attempted)) * avg_txn_value AS revenue_opportunity
  FROM decline_base
  JOIN market_config USING (market, carrier_id)
  GROUP BY 1, 2
)
SELECT *,
  SAFE_DIVIDE(retryable_declines, total_declines) AS retryable_rate,
  SAFE_DIVIDE(retries_attempted, retryable_declines) AS retry_coverage
FROM opportunity
ORDER BY revenue_opportunity DESC;

This query — run across each market and carrier combination — produced a ranked opportunity matrix. The output wasn't just which markets had low billthrough; it was which specific gaps had the highest recoverable revenue given retry success rates and transaction value. That's what determined intervention priority.

03 —

Interventions Designed & Tested

The diagnostic work produced a prioritized list of opportunities. The intervention design phase translated each opportunity into a testable change — whether to retry logic, billing timing, carrier routing, or merchant configuration. Each intervention was treated as an experiment with a defined hypothesis, measurement plan, and success threshold before any change went live.

01 —

Intelligent Retry Timing

Replaced fixed-interval retry schedules with timing models built on historical success probability by market, day of week, and billing cycle position. Retries for insufficient funds declines shifted to align with salary credit windows.

↑ +4.2pp billthrough contribution

02 —

Configuration Audit & Remediation

Systematic audit of billing parameters across all active merchant-carrier combinations. Identified misconfigured billing windows, incorrect currency mappings, and descriptor errors that were generating terminal declines on otherwise valid accounts.

↑ +2.1pp billthrough contribution

03 —

Carrier Routing Optimization

For markets with multiple carrier options, built a routing model that dynamically allocated volume based on real-time authorization rate performance and cost per successful transaction. Moved volume away from underperforming carriers during degraded periods.

↑ +1.7pp billthrough contribution

Most complex intervention — required real-time data pipeline and routing logic changes. Limited to top-5 markets by volume initially. Expanded after measured lift validated the model assumptions.

Measurement Challenge

Billthrough rate is not a clean A/B test — you can't randomly assign billing attempts to treatment and control when the same customer may have multiple attempts across a billing cycle. We addressed this through market-level holdouts and difference-in-differences analysis, comparing intervention markets against matched control markets on pre-intervention trend lines.

04 —

Measuring Results

Measurement was designed before any intervention deployed. The core challenge was attribution: with multiple interventions running sequentially across different markets, cleanly attributing lift to specific changes required discipline in rollout sequencing and control group maintenance.

The primary metric was net billthrough rate — successful authorizations as a percentage of total billing attempts, net of any retry costs. Secondary metrics included retry cost per recovered transaction, passive churn rate, and net revenue per active subscriber.

Measurement Approach — Difference in Differences

For each market where an intervention deployed, we selected a matched control market with similar pre-period billthrough trend, carrier mix, and merchant composition. The treatment effect was estimated as the difference in post-intervention trend between treatment and control, adjusted for any pre-existing divergence.

This approach isn't perfect — market-level events can create confounds — but it's the most credible approach available when randomization at the transaction level isn't feasible. We reported confidence intervals alongside point estimates and were explicit about the assumptions the measurement depended on.

05 —

Revenue Impact

The revenue impact of billthrough improvement compounds in subscription models in a way that makes point-in-time measurement understate the true value. A billing attempt that fails and doesn't recover doesn't just lose that transaction — it loses the subscriber, and therefore all future subscription revenue that subscriber would have generated.

We modeled impact across three horizons: immediate (transactions recovered in the billing cycle), short-term (passive churn reduction over 12 months), and long-term (LTV impact of retained subscribers).

Revenue Framing

A 1 percentage point improvement in billthrough rate across a portfolio of global subscription merchants processing hundreds of millions of billing attempts per year translates directly to hundreds of thousands of additional successful authorizations — each representing a subscription that continues rather than lapses. The compounding effect over a subscriber's lifetime makes the LTV impact a multiple of the immediate revenue figure.

After

Beyond the headline billthrough number, the program delivered two structural improvements that outlasted the initial interventions. First, the unified metrics framework became the operating standard for how the business measured and discussed payment performance — making future degradations detectable earlier and interventions faster to execute. Second, the retry and routing infrastructure built during the program created a platform for ongoing optimization rather than a one-time fix.

The most important outcome wasn't the 8pp improvement. It was building the analytical foundation that made the next improvement faster, cheaper, and more precise.

What I'd Do Differently

The diagnostic phase took longer than it needed to because we didn't have a unified decline taxonomy at the start. In future programs I'd invest in that infrastructure in week one rather than week four — the opportunity sizing would have been more credible earlier, and executive buy-in for the interventions would have come faster. The measurement framework also benefited from clearer upfront agreement on what "success" looked like before results came in, which reduced the post-hoc interpretation debates that slowed reporting.
